Computing for Society: YouTube

This sub-page of the Computing for Society website is dedicated to YouTube and outlines how YouTube affects society

YouTube


A Brief Overview


YouTube is an American video-sharing platform headquartered in San Bruno, California, created in February 2005. Google bought the site in November 2006 for US$1.65 billion.

YouTube allows users to upload, view, rate, share, add to playlists, report, comment on videos, and subscribe to other users.

According to the platform itself, YouTube's mission is "to give everyone a voice and show them the world".

The platform advocates for:

  • Freedom of Expression
  • Freedom of Information
  • Freedom Opportunity
  • Freedom to Belong

You can find out more on YouTube's history and features on Wikipedia

Quick Facts and Numbers


  • YouTube is the 2nd most popular social media platform in the world with 1.9 billion users

  • An estimated 400 hours of videos are uploaded on YouTube every day

  • On average, there are 1,000,000,000 mobile video views per day

  • In 2018, the 95% of the most watched videos were music videos

  • Searches of “how to” videos on YouTube are growing 70% year on year

  • The number of channels earning six figures each year on YouTube has increased by 40% year on year

Negative Effects on Viewers

Along with many positive influences on society listed below, YouTube has faced criticism over aspects of its operations, including:

  • Handling of copyrighted content contained within uploaded videos
  • Recommendation algorithms perpetuating videos that promote conspiracy theories and falsehoods
  • hosting videos ostensibly targeting children but containing violent and/or sexually suggestive content involving popular characters
  • Videos of minors attracting pedophilic activities in their comment sections
  • Fluctuating policies on the types of content that is eligible to be monetized with advertising

All bad aside, it is believed that an overall balance is positive and YouTube continues to improve many areas in societies, some of which are outlined below.

Proliferation of Knowledge


YouTube enables inexpensive creation and distribution of educational content, as well as "how to" videos created by individual YouTubers.

Worldwide video access has spurred innovation by enabling geographically distributed individuals to build upon each other's work and to collaborate.

One of the most important implications of enabling easier access to quality teaching materials and educators, is the opportunity to learn potentially life-changing skills, such as programming, completely free of charge.

Collaboration and Crowdsourcing


In projects such as the YouTube Symphony Orchestra and The Legion of Extraordinary Dancers, geographically distributed artists were selected based on their individual online video auditions and assembled on the same stage to perform, respectively, at Carnegie Hall (2009) and at the Academy Awards ceremonies (2010).

Alternative Ways of Making a Living


Enabling a new way of earning a livelihood, YouTube's "Partner Program" started in 2007 and by January 2012 grew to about 30,000 partners.

Top five hundred partners earn more than $100,000 annually each, and some earn considerably more.

Many popular brands were reported in 2012 to pay six figures direct to the most popular YouTubers to create and upload ads

What once restricted to popular individuals being able to afford an air time in traditional media, is now available to anyone with basic video equipment, creating tremendous opportunities for disadvantaged societies around the world.
